# study-of-seal-poop

A lighthearted but scientifically minded repository documenting observations, photos, and field notes on seal scat (poop, droppings, feces, scat) for educational and biological field-study purposes.

---

## Overview

This project collects descriptive notes, images, and interpretive guidance about seal scat encountered during coastal fieldwork. The goal is to provide an accessible, responsibly framed reference for:

- Naturalists and citizen scientists
- Undergraduate ecology and marine biology students
- Field instructors and volunteer survey teams

This repository is *not* a substitute for formal training or institutional protocols. It is intended to support observation and documentation (photography, metadata logging, descriptive morphology) rather than instruct on laboratory analysis.

---

## Why it matters

Seal scat is more than a funny roadside find — it's an informative, non-invasive window into marine ecosystems:

- Diet indicators: fish bones, squid beaks, crustacean remains reveal prey composition.
- Health monitoring: parasite load, unusual color/consistency, or debris can flag issues.
- Pollution signals: presence of plastics or anthropogenic debris points to contamination.
- Population and habitat use: scat locations help map foraging areas and haul-out site use.

Collectively, well-documented scat records support conservation, diet studies, and long-term monitoring with minimal disturbance to animals.

## Morphology notes

When observing and describing scat, consider standardized, objective descriptors. Helpful morphological attributes include:

- Shape and form: tubular, pelletized, amorphous, segmented
- Size: approximate length and maximum width (use a scale in photos when possible)
- Color and consistency: fresh dark/greenish vs weathered gray/brittle
- Contents visible by eye: fish bones, otoliths, squid beaks, shell fragments, hair, feathers
- Odor (describe qualitatively; avoid direct inhalation close-up)
- Associated signs: nearby regurgitates, tracks, fresh haul-out activity

Professional tip: use consistent terminology across observations. Example categories: Fresh/Tacky, Partially Weathered, Fibrous, Bone-rich, Plastics-present.

## Habitat / Diet clues

Interpreting scat content can yield habitat and dietary insights:

- Many coastal species: fish bones and small otoliths indicate benthic or pelagic fish prey.
- Squid beaks: suggest offshore or nocturnal foraging behavior, depending on regional squid species.
- Crustacean exoskeletons or shell fragments: intertidal or nearshore feeding.
- Feather fragments: occasional seabird predation or scavenging.
- Plastics/anthropogenic debris: direct evidence of marine pollution and ingestion risk.

Caveat: appearances are suggestive, not definitive. For rigorous dietary analysis (e.g., DNA metabarcoding, stable isotopes), follow institutional laboratory procedures and permitting requirements.

---

## Ethics & safety

Safety and ethical practice are essential.

- Do not approach or disturb seals to obtain scat: maintain appropriate distance and follow marine mammal disturbance guidelines.
- Treat all scat as potentially biohazardous:
  - Use gloves when handling or placing scales/markers near samples.
  - Avoid touching eyes/face and wash hands thoroughly afterward.
- Do not collect or transport biological samples without permits and institutional approval.
- Respect local laws and protected-area rules; obtain necessary permits for sampling or removal.
- If you encounter stranded or injured animals, contact local wildlife authorities rather than intervening directly.

Responsible observation prioritizes animal welfare and researcher safety. When in doubt, photograph and record metadata rather than manipulate samples.
